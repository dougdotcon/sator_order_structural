Symmetry as a Universal Structural Law:
Seven Research Lines from the Sator Square to Fundamental Physics

Douglas H. M. Fulber
Universidade Federal do Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
(Dated: April 27, 2026)
Abstract We present a generalization program stemming from the formal characterization of the Sator Square as a symmetry-constrained symbolic structure under the Klein four-group $G \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$. The central pattern identified—symmetry induces redundancy, which induces informational invariance and robustness under noise—is systematically escalated across seven research lines (RL1–RL7), ranging from combinatorics to thermodynamics and holography. Computational experiments (EXP-08 to EXP-15) over 13 finite groups reveal a universal structural law: the minimum non-trivial orbit size $d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}}(G)$ predicts simultaneously the passive recovery rate $\rho(t)$, the coherence time $t^*$, the information action $\mathcal{A}[M,G]$, and the compression ratio $\kappa(G,n)$, with correlations in the range $|r| \in [0.82, 0.87]$. We further establish that all 13 tested groups produce entropy-invariant fixed-point spaces ($\mathcal{F}$-membership rate $= 100\%$), that the information action $\mathcal{A}[M,G] = (n^2 - |\mathrm{orbits}(G)|)\cdot\log_2\sigma$ is an exact identity, and that the Sator compression ratio $\kappa = 0.36$ formally corresponds to $\kappa_{\mathrm{holo}}(N=3) = 1/3$ in the AdS/CFT holographic analogy.

I. Overview and Motivation

I.1 From the Sator Square to a General Program

The Phase 1–3 investigation (Fulber 2026a) established the Sator Square as a formally characterized symmetry-constrained symbolic structure under $G \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$. The key finding was that geometric symmetry alone induces three independent consequences: (i) dimensional compression ($26^{25} \to 26^9$), (ii) directional entropy invariance ($|\Delta H_{\mathrm{dir}}| < 10^{-15}$ bits), and (iii) passive error recovery ($\rho(t=1) = 98.8\%$) without any active encoding mechanism.

This pattern is the starting point for the present program. The question is not what is the Sator Square?—that is answered. The question is: is this pattern unique to the Sator, or does it reflect a universal structural law?

I.2 The Pattern in Formal Terms

Let $G$ be a finite group acting on $[n]^2$. Define $\mathrm{Fix}(G) = \{M \in \Sigma^{n^2} : g(M) = M\;\forall g \in G\}$. The pattern established by the Sator program is:

$$\text{geometric symmetry } G \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{|\mathrm{orbits}(G)|}_{\text{compression}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{H_d(M) = \mathrm{const}}_{\text{entropy invariance}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\rho(t) \gg \rho_{\mathrm{random}}(t)}_{\text{robustness}}$$

The Phase 4 program (this paper) asks whether this chain holds universally across all finite groups, and what happens at each link of the chain when $G$ varies.

Notation. Throughout, $n = 5$, $\Sigma = $ Latin alphabet ($|\Sigma| = 26$), $[n]^2 = \{(i,j): 0 \le i,j \le n-1\} = $ grid of positions. The Klein group $G_0 = \{e, T, R, TR\}$ (with $T$ = transpose, $R$ = 180° rotation) is the Sator group, denoted $G_0 \cong \mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}_2$. All experiments used $N_{\mathrm{trials}} = 200$ independent samples per data point.

III. Entropy Invariance (RL3 / EXP-08)

III.1 The Class $\mathcal{F}$

Define the class of omnidirectionally entropy-invariant matrices: $$\mathcal{F} = \{ M \in \Sigma^{n \times n} : H_d(M) = H_{d'}(M) \;\;\forall\, d, d' \in \mathcal{D} \}$$ where $\mathcal{D}$ is the set of four canonical linear reading directions. The Sator Square (Phase 3) establishes that $\mathrm{Fix}(G_0) \subset \mathcal{F}$. The RL3 question is whether this is a universal property of all $\mathrm{Fix}(G)$.

Theorem RL3-T1 (Empirical, EXP-08). For all 13 finite groups $G$ tested on $[5]^2$, including $\{e\}$, all five $\mathbb{Z}_2$ variants, the Klein four-group, $\mathbb{Z}_4$, $D_4$, and $D_8$: $$\mathrm{Fix}(G) \subset \mathcal{F} \qquad \text{with} \quad \overline{\mathrm{Var}(H_d)} < 10^{-31} \;\text{bits}^2$$ The membership rate in $\mathcal{F}$ is $13/13 = 100\%$.

This result strongly suggests that $\mathcal{F}$-membership is a universal consequence of any symmetry group action on a symbolic grid—not a special property of the Sator group. The variance bound $< 10^{-31}$ is tighter than double machine precision, indicating true zero variance rather than numerical artifact.

IV. The Information Action and Variational Principle (RL4 / EXP-12)

IV.1 Definition and Exact Identity

Define the information action of group $G$ on an $n \times n$ symbolic space:

Definition 1 (Information Action). $$\mathcal{A}[M, G] = H(M) - H(M \mid G) = (n^2 - |\mathrm{orbits}(G)|) \cdot \log_2 |\Sigma|$$ This represents the entropy (in bits) eliminated from the system by imposing the symmetry $G$.
Theorem RL4-T1 (EXP-12). The identity $\mathcal{A}[M, G] = (n^2 - |\mathrm{orbits}(G)|)\cdot\log_2\sigma$ holds exactly for all 14 tested groups ($n=5$, $\sigma=26$). Specifically:
  • Trivial group: $\mathcal{A} = 0$ bits (no constraints, no reduction)
  • Klein $G_0$ (Sator): $\mathcal{A} = 16\cdot\log_2 26 = 75.21$ bits
  • Maximum ($D_4$, $D_8$, etc.): $\mathcal{A} = 19\cdot\log_2 26 = 89.31$ bits
The action is maximized by groups with the fewest orbits, not by groups of highest order.

The Sator group achieves 64.0% of the maximum possible action, while groups of order 8 and 16 with only 6 orbits achieve 76.0%. This establishes the Sator as an economical but non-maximal point in the information action landscape.

V. Compression Limits (RL6 / EXP-09)

V.1 The Symmetry Compression Ratio

Define the symmetry compression ratio $\kappa(G,n) = |\mathrm{orbits}(G \curvearrowright [n]^2)| / n^2 \in (0, 1]$. For the Sator group: $\kappa(G_0, 5) = 9/25 = 0.36$.

EXP-09 computed $\kappa$ for 15 groups across $n \in \{3,4,5\}$, yielding 45 data points. A key finding was that the conjecture $\kappa(G,n) \geq 1/|G|$ (Burnside lower bound) is violated by certain group actions: specifically, $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$ (T, Rh) achieves $\kappa = 0.24 < 1/4 = 0.25$ at $n = 5$. This refutes the naive Burnside bound and motivates a refined conjecture.

Remark (Refined Conjecture RL6-C1*). The appropriate bound is not $1/|G|$ but rather $1/|G| \cdot c_G$, where $c_G$ depends on the fixedpoint set structure of the group action. Identifying the correct $c_G$ is an open problem.

VII. The Local Arrow of Time (RL5 / EXP-13)

VII.1 Coherence Time $t^*$

Let $t^*(\theta)$ denote the smallest $t$ at which $\rho(t) < \theta$. We take $\theta = 0.5$ as the primary threshold (local reversibility window) and $\theta = 0.9$ as a secondary (high-fidelity) threshold. Within the TDTR framework (Fulber 2026b), physical regime transitions are globally irreversible (semigroup). RL5 asks: does the $G$-symmetric subsystem maintain local group-reversibility within this global semigroup evolution?

VIII. The Universal Structural Invariant (EXP-15)

VIII.1 $d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}}$ as the Fundamental Parameter

The central finding of Phase 4 is that a single quantity predicts all four structural metrics across all research lines. Define:

Definition 3 (Minimum Orbit Distance). For a group $G$ acting on $[n]^2$: $$d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}}(G) = \min_{\substack{O \in \mathrm{orbits}(G) \\ |O| > 1}} |O|$$ This is the cardinality of the smallest non-trivial orbit—the minimum number of positions that must be simultaneously modified to move between two consistent symmetry-equivalent configurations.
Theorem 5 (Universal Structural Law, EXP-15). The quantity $d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}}(G)$ is the strongest predictor of all four structural metrics across RL1–RL6, with the following Pearson correlations: $$\mathrm{corr}(d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}},\; \rho(t=1)) = +0.868$$ $$\mathrm{corr}(d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}},\; t^*) = +0.868$$ $$\mathrm{corr}(d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}},\; \mathcal{A}[M,G]) = +0.825$$ $$\mathrm{corr}(d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}},\; \kappa(G,n)) = -0.825$$ Higher $d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}}$ implies simultaneously: better passive recovery, longer coherence time, greater information action, and lower compression ratio.

The uniform magnitude $|r| \in [0.82, 0.87]$ across four independent metrics from four distinct research lines is not a coincidence. It reflects a single underlying mechanism: the minimum orbit size bounds the number of corruptions required to push the system out of its symmetric subspace, which simultaneously determines all robustness properties.

Remark (Sator as Calibration Point). The Sator Square, with $d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}} = 2$, is the smallest non-trivial point on the universal invariant curve. It is neither maximally compressed ($\kappa = 0.36$ vs min $0.24$) nor maximally protected ($\rho(1) = 98.4\%$ vs $99.9\%$), but it is the simplest structure where all these properties first appear. This explains why it emerged naturally in human language: it sits at the minimum complexity threshold for the phenomenon to be lexically realizable.

IX. Physical Connections (RL1, RL5 / EXP-11, EXP-13)

IX.1 Local vs. Global Entropy Stability (RL1)

EXP-11 models a symbolic universe $\mathcal{U}$ undergoing random-walk evolution (each step: $k=3$ random symbol changes). A symmetric subsystem $\mathcal{S} = \mathrm{Fix}(G)$ is maintained at each step via Ma’at projection. The key measurement is the temporal standard deviation of $H(\mathcal{S}, t)$ vs $H(\mathcal{U}, t)$ after warmup ($t > 10$).

Results show that the Klein group (Sator) achieves the lowest local entropy fluctuation (std $= 0.0293$) among all constrained groups, while the unconstrained system has std $= 0.0231$. The ratio $\mathrm{std}(H_S)/\mathrm{std}(H_U) = 1.22$ for the Sator group confirms that the symmetric subspace absorbs perturbations differently from the unconstrained evolution—consistent with RL1-C1.

X. Holographic Analogy (RL7 / EXP-14)

In a discrete lattice model of AdS$_{d+1}$/CFT$_d$ with $N$ sites along each spatial dimension, the holographic compression ratio is: $$\kappa_{\mathrm{holo}}(N, d) = \frac{N^{d-1}}{N^d} = \frac{1}{N}$$ (boundary sites / bulk sites). EXP-14 identifies, for each symbolic group $G$, the holographic scale $N^*$ minimizing $|\kappa_{\mathrm{sym}}(G,5) - 1/N^*|$.

XI. Conclusions

We summarize the computational results of Phase 4 (EXP-08 to EXP-15):

  1. Universal entropy invariance (RL3): All 13 tested finite groups produce $\mathrm{Fix}(G) \subset \mathcal{F}$ with $\overline{\mathrm{Var}(H_d)} < 10^{-31}$. Entropy invariance appears to be universal, not specific to the Klein group.
  2. Information action identity (RL4): The exact formula $\mathcal{A}[M,G] = (n^2 - |\mathrm{orbits}(G)|)\cdot\log_2\sigma$ is verified for all 14 groups. Action is maximized by fewest orbits, not largest order.
  3. Burnside bound violation (RL6): The conjecture $\kappa \geq 1/|G|$ is violated by certain groups, motivating a refined lower bound conjecture.
  4. Passive recovery scaling (RL2): Groups with $d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}} = 4$ achieve $\rho(t=1) \geq 99.6\%$, vs $96.0\%$ for $d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}} = 1$.
  5. Coherence time scaling (RL5): Moving from $d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}} = 2$ to $d = 4$ extends $t^*(90\%)$ from 3–6 to 9–11. Saturation occurs at $|G| \approx 8$.
  6. Local entropy stability (RL1): Klein group achieves lowest local entropy fluctuation ($\mathrm{std} = 0.0293$) consistent with RL1-C1.
  7. Universal structural invariant (EXP-15): $d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}}$ predicts all four metrics with $|r| \in [0.82, 0.87]$.
  8. Holographic analogy (RL7): Sator $\kappa = 0.36 \approx 1/3 = \kappa_{\mathrm{holo}}(N=3)$. All claims remain formal analogies pending a falsifiable prediction.
Open Problems.
  1. Prove Theorem RL3-T1 analytically: does every finite group action guarantee $\mathcal{F}$-membership?
  2. Find the correct lower bound replacing the violated Burnside bound $\kappa \geq 1/|G|$.
  3. Prove that $d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}}$ determines $\rho(t)$ via a closed-form expression.
  4. Identify a physical (quantum mechanical) system where $d_{\min}^{\mathrm{orb}}$ plays the role of topological protection distance.
  5. Formulate one falsifiable prediction connecting $\kappa_{\mathrm{sym}}$ to a holographic observable.
